The Difference That Matters

Penetrating Stain vs.
Surface Film — Why It Matters

Not all stains are the same. Most deck stain you buy at a hardware store is a film-forming product — it sits on top of the wood and peels within 2 seasons. We use penetrating stains that soak into the wood fibers.

💧
Penetrates Into the Fiber — Doesn't Peel
Because it's inside the wood, not on top, there's nothing to peel, chip, or flake. No film to crack when the wood expands and contracts.
❄
Survives Freeze-Thaw Cycles
Water repellency at the fiber level means moisture can't penetrate, freeze, and split your decking. The wood breathes but stays protected.
🌞
UV Stabilizers Prevent Graying
Premium stains include UV blockers that slow the natural graying and silvering process. Your deck holds color longer between maintenance cycles.
⏱
Easier Maintenance
When it's time to re-stain in 3–5 years, penetrating stains don't need stripping. Clean, prep, and apply — a fraction of the work of a film product.

The Staining Process

5 Steps to a Deck That Lasts a Decade

Prep is 80% of the result. We don't skip steps because shortcuts show up in year two.

1
Power Wash & Strip
Full power wash to remove dirt, mold, mildew, and old surface coating. Stripping applied if existing finish is failing. Clean wood is the starting point.
2
Sand & Repair
Rough spots sanded smooth. Raised grain knocked down. Loose boards tightened. Any damage flagged and repaired before stain goes on.
3
Apply Premium Stain
First coat of penetrating stain applied in the right conditions — temperature and humidity matter. We don't rush the application or cut corners on coverage.
4
Second Coat (if needed)
High-traffic areas and weathered wood often need a second coat for full saturation. We assess after the first coat dries and apply where needed.
5
Sealer Finish
A final sealer coat on high-exposure surfaces locks in the stain and adds an additional barrier against moisture and UV. The last line of defense.
